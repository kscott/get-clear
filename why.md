# Why Get Clear

## The moment it landed

I was trying to name a suite of command-line tools I'd been building. Five tools — Reminders, Calendar, Contacts, Mail, Messages — all designed to work with Claude as the interface.

I went through a long list of words. Accord (too Honda). Pact (too governmental). Thread, Keep, Poise. None of them fit.

Then the frame shifted.

The suite isn't about tools. It's about the people you're working with and the promises made to them. Once that was clear, the name followed: **Get Clear**. Not a state — a movement. The work of getting there.

And then the origin: *I want to be doing better. And I want to share how I'm doing better.*

That sentence is the whole project.

---

## Who this is for

The managers. The collaborators. The ones keeping the process going, holding relationships together, trying to honor every commitment while the calendar fills and the inbox grows.

Not developers — they have plenty of tools built by people who understand their problem. This is for the person herding cats. The one who ends the day wondering if they got back to everyone, if anything slipped, if they kept their word.

That person wants to feel balanced, in control, on top of things. They want their mind clear and their heart light. They want more time for what they actually love — which is usually the people, not the process.

---

## Why Claude at the center

A traditional CLI tool is built for a human typing commands in a terminal. That's not this.

Get Clear is built for Claude to operate on your behalf. You have a conversation. Claude translates it into actions. The meeting gets added. The email goes out. The reminder is set.

That changes the design. Claude has read the record before touching it. The conversation is the undo log. "Oops, that wasn't right" is a complete recovery instruction. Commands can be softer — *add, remove, change* — because the safety net is the relationship, not the word choice.

You're not issuing commands to a machine. You're talking to a capable colleague who handles the details.

---

## The feedback loop

Using the tools is half the story. Knowing what you did is the other half.

Every action — every reminder added, every email sent, every commitment kept — gets logged automatically. At the end of the day you can ask: *what did I get done today?* And the answer is there, not reconstructed from memory, but recorded as it happened.

The done list isn't a report. It's affirmation. You did these things. You kept these commitments. You moved these things forward.

That's motivation. That's the thing that makes you want to do it again tomorrow.

Without the log, Get Clear helps you do things. With the log, it shows you who you're becoming.

---

## The itch

The best tools come out of an itch. This one does.

Building the CLI pieces raised the itch and satisfied it at the same time. The tool shaped the understanding of the problem as it was built. That doesn't happen with a speculative product — it happens when you're solving something real.

I built this because I needed it. I'm sharing it because I think others need it too. Somewhere out there is a manager at 11pm, behind on three follow-ups, who will read the first sentence and think — *that's me* — and feel a little less alone in it.

That's enough reason to share.

---

*Get clear. Stay clear. All clear.*
